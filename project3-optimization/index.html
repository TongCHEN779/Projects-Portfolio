▚ THE MOMENT / SOS HIERARCHY

Each order is one step higher.

Certifying the global minimum of a multivariate polynomial is NP-hard, so we solve moment/SOS (semidefinite) relaxations. Each one gives a rigorous lower bound — and every time you raise the order, the bound climbs one step closer to the true minimum. Watch it as a 3-D surface below (rotate with your mouse); the green floor is the bound climbing the staircase.

Gradual gap (cubic region). Minimizing 0.4x²−y²+0.3xy−0.2x−0.6y over the patch carved by y ≥ x³−x, y ≤ 1.2, x²+y² ≤ 4. Lasserre's order r=2 gives a loose floor; r=3 rises to touch the minimum. A textbook order-by-order climb.

Motzkin — the famous one. M(x,y)=x⁴y²+x²y⁴−3x²y²+1 is ≥ 0 everywhere (four zeros at (±1,±1)) yet is not a sum of squares (Hilbert 1888, Motzkin 1967). So plain SOS reports an absurd ≈ −10 for a polynomial whose true minimum is 0 — then a single level of the hierarchy (multiplying by (1+x²+y²)) jumps straight to exact. This one gap is the entire reason the SOS/moment hierarchy has to exist.

Honest note. The bounds were computed offline with a real SDP solver (cvxpy/SCS) — there's no semidefinite solver in the browser — while the surfaces are evaluated live. This is my research area, and the same "is this polynomial nonnegative on this set?" machinery underpins certified adversarial robustness in Project 1.
